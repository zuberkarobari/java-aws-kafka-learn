Behavioural Interview Prep — STAR Stories, Leadership and Communication

STAR Method

Situation, Task, Action, Result for impact

Common Questions

Leadership, conflict, failure, initiative

Senior Signals

What senior-level answers sound like

Practice

Your written answers + out-loud practice

Note:

Behavioural questions at senior level are not about whether you have the right stories. They are about whether your answers show ownership, clear thinking under pressure, and awareness of impact beyond just your own work. Every answer should end with a concrete result and a reflection on what you learned.

The STAR Format for Senior Engineers

Element

What senior-level answers include

Situation

Specific context — team size, system scale, business impact if it failed. Not 'I was working on a project' but 'I was the lead backend engineer on a payment service processing 1M transactions per day'

Task

What was specifically yours to own — not what the team did. 'I was responsible for diagnosing and fixing the latency issue before the client review the next morning'

Action

Three to four specific technical decisions with reasoning. Not 'I fixed it' but 'I added distributed tracing, found the slow span, identified a missing index, added it with CREATE INDEX CONCURRENTLY to avoid table lock'

Result

Quantified outcome: latency dropped from 8s to 340ms, zero incidents in 3 months since, approach adopted by two other teams. Plus: what you learned or would do differently

Common Behavioural Questions with Senior-Level Guidance

Tell me about a time you disagreed with a technical decision.

Senior signal:

Shows you can advocate for the right technical direction while maintaining professional relationships. The answer should end with either the decision changing based on your reasoning, or you implementing the decision you disagreed with professionally after making your case.

Your answer:

Describe a time you had to meet a tight deadline. How did you handle it?

Senior signal:

Not about working extra hours. About: prioritising the essential over the nice-to-have, communicating clearly about what would and would not be done, and delivering something that worked rather than something perfect. Shows judgement, not just effort.

Your answer:

Tell me about a time you made a mistake. What happened?

Senior signal:

Interviewers are testing self-awareness and whether you learn from failures. The worst answer is denying mistakes. The best answer: name the mistake clearly without excuses, explain what went wrong technically, describe the impact honestly, and then explain exactly what you changed so it would not happen again.

Your answer:

How do you explain a complex technical concept to a non-technical stakeholder?

Senior signal:

Give a real example. The best answers describe using analogy, checking for understanding, and adapting to the audience's concern — not the technical detail, but the business impact of the technical decision.

Your answer:

Tell me about a time you took initiative to improve something that was not your responsibility.

Senior signal:

Shows ownership beyond your ticket scope. The best stories: you noticed a systemic problem (not just a bug), proposed a solution with business rationale, got buy-in from the right people, and the improvement had measurable impact.

Questions to Ask the Interviewer

Why this matters:

The questions you ask at the end signal what kind of engineer you are. Asking about oncall burden, team culture, or how decisions are made tells the interviewer you think about working conditions seriously. Asking about the tech stack alone signals you are junior.

Question

What it signals about you

How does the team decide on technical architecture — is it driven by one person or a collaborative process?

You care about technical culture and whether you will have influence

What does a production incident look like for this team — how does the oncall rotation work and how do you do postmortems?

You are thinking seriously about operational realities

What is the biggest technical challenge the team is facing right now?

You want to understand the real work, not just the job description

How does the team approach technical debt — is there space to improve things alongside feature work?

You care about code quality long-term

What would the first three months look like for someone in this role?

Shows you are thinking about impact and ramp-up, not just the job offer

What has changed most about how the team works in the last year?

Reveals team adaptability and whether the culture is improving or stagnant
